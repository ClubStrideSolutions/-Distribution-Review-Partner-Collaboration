"Hunger = Stress = Risk" Campaign Framework

Core Message:

"When basic needs aren't met, everything else becomes harder. Let's break the cycle together."

Key Statistics to Share:

  • Teens experiencing food insecurity are 2x more likely to report anxiety and depression
  • Chronic stress from hunger affects decision-making and increases risky behaviors
  • Food-insecure youth are 1.5x more likely to engage in unprotected sex
  • Free resources exist, but only 30% of eligible youth access them

Campaign Pillars:

  1. RECOGNIZE: The connections between hunger, stress, and health risks
  2. REDUCE: Stigma around seeking help for any of these issues
  3. RESOURCE: Connect peers to free, confidential services

Youth-Friendly Mental Health Messaging

Do Say:

  • "It's okay to not be okay"
  • "Your feelings are valid"
  • "Help is available, no questions asked"
  • "You deserve support"
  • "Mental health is health"

Don't Say:

  • ‚ùå "Just think positive"
  • ‚ùå "Others have it worse"
  • ‚ùå "You're being dramatic"
  • ‚ùå "Snap out of it"
  • ‚ùå Clinical terms without explanation

STI Prevention Messaging Guide

Key Messages:

  • "Your health, your choice, your privacy" - Emphasize confidentiality for teens 12+
  • "Free testing, no judgment" - Remove financial and social barriers
  • "Knowledge is power" - Focus on empowerment, not fear
  • "Stress affects decisions" - Connect to broader health picture

Conversation Starters:

  • "Did you know you can get tested for free without parent permission if you're 12+?"
  • "Stress from life stuff can affect our health choices - let's talk about resources"
  • "Taking care of your sexual health is just as important as other health checkups"

Visual Concepts:

  • üéØ Infographic: "3 Steps to Free, Confidential Testing"
  • üì± QR code cards: "Scan for Nearest Testing Location"
  • ü§ù Peer testimonials: "Why I Got Tested" (anonymous)
  • üìç Map visual: "5 Free Testing Sites Near You"

Important Reminders:

  • Always include that services are FREE and CONFIDENTIAL
  • Provide specific locations and hours
  • Include multiple ways to access (walk-in, appointment, hotline)
  • Show that staff are youth-friendly and non-judgmental

School Distribution Flyer Review 25 mins

  • Audit current distribution
  • Identify gaps and successes
  • Document with photos
+

Key Terms

Distribution audit: Systematic review of where materials have been placed and their current status.

Coverage gaps: Areas or populations that haven't received materials as planned.

High-traffic zones: School locations with maximum student visibility and engagement.

Discussion Points

  • Which locations have been most effective for flyer visibility?
  • What feedback have you received from peers about the materials?
  • How can we ensure materials reach diverse student groups?
  • What challenges have you faced in distribution?

Activity

Part 1 (10 mins): Individual review and documentation:

  • Complete the distribution tracking sheet for your assigned areas
  • Take photos of current flyer placements
  • Note which locations need replenishment
  • Record any damaged or removed materials

Part 2 (10 mins): Team sharing and gap analysis:

  • Share findings with your team
  • Identify patterns in successful vs. unsuccessful placements
  • Map underserved areas or student populations
  • Create action plan for next distribution round

Part 3 (5 mins): Full group debrief on best practices discovered.

Reflection Questions

  • What surprised you about where materials were most/least effective?
  • How can we better reach students who might be hesitant to pick up health information?
  • What adjustments should we make for the next distribution phase?

Key Terms

Community partners: Organizations or individuals outside school who share our health advocacy goals.

School allies: Staff members who can amplify our message (wellness centers, counselors, food services).

Value proposition: Clear statement of mutual benefit from collaboration.

Champion: An influential person who actively supports and promotes your cause.

Discussion Points

  • Who in your community already cares about youth health issues?
  • Which school staff members have shown interest in student wellness?
  • What organizations address food insecurity, mental health, or STI prevention locally?
  • How can partnerships strengthen our campaign's reach and credibility?

"Hunger = Stress = Risk" Campaign Design 35 mins

  • Develop campaign messaging
  • Focus on mental health & STIs
  • Create action plan
+

Key Terms

Syndemic: When multiple health issues interact and reinforce each other (food insecurity + stress + STI risk).

Awareness campaign: Coordinated efforts to educate and change attitudes about health issues.

Peer messaging: Health information delivered by and for young people in relatable language.

Call to action: Clear steps people can take after learning about an issue.

Discussion Points

  • How does hunger increase stress and risky behaviors among youth?
  • Why is it important to address these issues together rather than separately?
  • What makes mental health and STI topics particularly challenging to discuss?
  • How can we make these connections clear without overwhelming people?

Activity

Part 1 (10 mins): Understanding the connections:

  • Review the "Hunger = Stress = Risk" concept from program materials
  • Discuss how food insecurity leads to:
    • Increased mental health challenges (anxiety, depression)
    • Higher stress levels affecting decision-making
    • Greater vulnerability to risky behaviors including unprotected sex
  • Brainstorm real examples from Vallejo youth experiences

Part 2 (20 mins): Campaign development:

  • Choose a specific focus within the food-stress-risk connection
  • Develop campaign elements:
    • Core message (1-2 sentences)
    • 3 key facts to share
    • Visual concepts (poster ideas, social media)
    • Peer-to-peer talking points
    • Resources to promote (use the Vallejo Youth Health Directory)
    • Specific mental health and STI services to highlight
  • Plan one awareness activity (lunch table, assembly, social media push)

Part 3 (5 mins): Quick pitch - each team shares their campaign concept.

Reflection Questions

  • How does connecting these issues change how we approach health education?
  • What resistance might we face, and how can we address it sensitively?
  • How will your campaign reduce stigma around mental health and STI testing?
